TEXTURING A CAR
Written by Benjamin "Tora_2097" Brosdau

This is a tutorial showing the process of arranging textures in a 2D application and applying them in a 3D package. The entire process from the very beginning to the final adjustment of the shaders is covered.
You need the following software(s) to follow the tutorial:
- a 2D paint application able to deal with layer based workflow. (I used Photoshop here)
- a 3D application (almost every common package will be just fine here, I used 3ds max 6.0 along with Mental Ray to complete the task).
- a copy of "Total Textures Vol. 8: Cars".

I assume that you are familiar with the most common tools in Photoshop and your 3D application (just named „3ds max"from here on.) You do not need any artistic skills in painting although it is never a bad thing in case you have them. J
The two images above show the end result of this tutorial, so let's just start with getting our textures ready in Photoshop.

As you can see here I went away from the polished look of high gloss coated cars, but instead I decided to create a dirty/ muddy look on our car using the dirtmaps included on the Total Textures CD.
I created a new document placing the textures in a way that they fit nicely along with each other.
Note: Most of the textures that come along with the CD are very well tileable but I wanted to give you a short look at how things can be touched up early in the creation process and how you can create unique looking textures in just a few steps.
So let us just fill out the gaps in the texture that will later be covering the side of the car.

Just use your eyedropper tool to grab the needed color and start to fill in the space with a quite noisy brush type. Go on with adding darker spots trying to imitate the look of the original map.

That's how it looked just after a few minutes painting in Photoshop.

Use the clone stamp tool to copy/paste in the missing dots/ splats right in between the two maps.
That looks already quite satisfying but the straight border on the top really distracts from a realistic look.


We will work around that problem by just giving that border some randomness by using the eraser tool.

Just one more gap to fill out here, all the techniques that you have just used will come in very handy on this task.

That is my result after just a few minutes more. That should already be a nice dirtmap on our car, but I want to go one step further, I actually would like to see some race numbers on the door that will partially be covered by the mud on the side of the car. And this is the process of how to create such an effect: Make a copy of your base layer and choose any race number you wish to use on your car.

Use the magic wand tool to select all the white areas of the map and invert your selection.

Now simply drag and drop your selection just between your base layer and its copy.

Mark all the black areas of the top layer by again making use of the magic wand tool. You might want to use rather low pixel tolerance values here.

Next step is to delete your selection and the underlying race number will appear but still being partially covered by the remaining muddy part of the above layer.
We leave the map at this stage for just a few minutes and will now move on to 3ds max to prepare the car for the mapping process.

That is the car I will be using further on in this tutorial.

Hide everything except the cars body and create a new blend material in the material editor. I already have created the base shader for the car which is a simple raytrace material. I included the setting for my base material in the following image:

A few notes on the shader though: using specific falloffs on the diffuse and reflect channel is very common method, but I wanted to achieve the certain look of metallic flakes inside the paint surface. In case you have a 3rd party plugin like Splutterfish Brazil this a very easy task since it features that stuff right out of the box.
In case you don't have it just apply the above settings to your shader. The idea behind it is to affect the specular level of the material in such a way that is shows areas with very high specular (i.e. the metal flakes) and other areas with lower specular- the remaining car paint.
It is also possible to use any of the shaders included on the Total Texture CD as well of course.
But back to our newly created blend material. Fill both of its slots with a raytrace material and leave everything else at default settings.

Move to level 0 of your mesh and select all the faces that defines the side of car. Now add a UVW map modifier to the step WITHOUT de-selecting your faces. Since the cars side is an almost planar surface we will do just fine with a simple planar projection type. Don't forget to apply your blend material to the polygon selection at this stage.

Add an Unwrap UVW modifier to stack and click on the "Edit" button. You should see an result similar to the image above.

Now go back to the material editor and drag/drop a copy of your cars base shader in the first slot, move to the diffuse channel of the second slot and put in the map you have just painted in Photoshop. Click on the "Show map in viewport" button to be able to see your result.


You will most likely find out that the map does not fit perfectly to the shape of your cars body. And that is the time when our Unwrap UVW modifier will become useful.
You just have to pull/push the points in the Edit UVWs window and you'll see the viewport updating in realtime. Adjust the map to your needs now.
It might also occur to you that your decals or -in our case- race number does not sit in the correct place. If that's the case head back to Photoshop and simply move only the middle layer of your entire map and save your changes and see how 3ds max will update to those changes immediately. I had to adjust the size and position in my case. Once you are satisfied with the placement you should flatten the image in Photoshop and safe it as a TGA file for example.

Take this map, desaturate it and increase the contrast to a maximum. Safe it under a different name, go back to 3ds max and put the just created map into the "mask" slot of your blend material. Now it is time for a first testrender of our car.

Well, that looks pretty much like the side of a car covered in mud to me. Note: I already have applied a bump map to the dirtmap in the image. To create a sufficient bumpmap in Photoshop you should hide the layer with your decals/ racenumbers etc. because you will probably not want to see them embossed on the cars surface. Flatten the image then and desaturate it in order to get a grayscale image. Use this map in the bumpmap channel. You may want to play around in Photoshop to achieve certain results in 3ds max. For example try other decals, deal with lower or higher contrast settings to affect the coverage rate of the dirt etc. But basically this is the process of placing maps, decals, sponsors, numbers, flames, tribals etc. on your cars surface. To keep things easy here I just explained this process on a small portion of the car. In case you have hundreds of decals to place, it would be a very painful job to select a few polys everytime, apply a blend material to it, unwrap it and so on and on. In such a case you would probably want to unwrap the car as a whole and create a single surface map in Photoshop. The setup of such a map would of course take you longer than this one here but in the end it is much more efficient.

I went on a little further and placed a few other maps on the cars surface. I wanted to achieve the look of a car that has just been out racing on a muddy racetrack.

Here is a quickly rendered image of my car so far. Finally I want to put this car in a little scene for a more realistic look.

Here is the scene I set up for this tutorial. As you can see in the images there is nothing very fancy going on there. Modeling is quite simply and the texturing is -except the ground- pretty straight forward with just diffuse and bump mapping. To make the cars muddy appearance a little more believable I covered the ground partially with water puddles. The technique for this is again the same as for surfacing the car. A blend material, one of the slots containing the dry map, the other one containing a reflective water shader mixed together by a procedural noise map in the mask slot. The scene has been lit with an area light and final gather only.

This is just a quick testrender without antialiasing, you can see the final result on the top of this tutorial.

Well, that is pretty much it for this tutorial, I hope you found it useful in some way.
I haven't discovered any new texturing phenomena in here but it should give you a good look on how to use textures in general and the ones on this Texture CD in special. But there are not only a lot of very useful textures on this CD instead you will also find pre-made rims, paintshaders and other stuff like that on it. I used a set of those rims on the studio-like render on the top.
I recommend this CD to everyone who needs to do high quality renders of cars and other vehicles, because you find some textures and samples on this CD that are indeed very hard to find elsewhere.
